    Oncology¶

    • Palliative chemotherapy: patient is not
    • Curative chemotherapy: Adjuvant chemotherapy vs Neo-adjuvant chemotherapy
    • Neoadjuvant therapy encompasses all treatments that are administered before the primary cancer treatment, whereas adjuvant therapy describes regimens administered after the primary treatment.

    Paraneoplastic Conditions¶

    Divide into general conditions that applies for most cancers and some cancer specific problems

    Non-specific symptoms¶

    • coma and death
    • neuropsychiatric conditions
    • GI disturbances
    • renal failure

    Cachexia¶

    This is itself a syndrome, characterized by loss of body weight of 10%, reduced food intake and systemic inflammation

    • Adipose tissue loss due to Zinc-α2-glycoprotein, inhibition of lipoprotein lipase by cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1, IFN-α),Cori cycle is the main metablism pathway of tumor and is only performing glycolysis, Uncoupling proteins UCP1-UCP5 in mitochondrial inner mmb results in a lack of ATP production
    • Decreased protein synthesis not corrected by dietary supplementation plus increased protein catabolism; Proteolysis-inducing factor by tumor, cytokines (TNF-α, IFN-α); Activate NF-κβ and proteosome degradation ubiquitination of myofibrillar proteins

    Anemia¶

    Chronic production of acute phase markers, IL-6 decreases iron circulation and TNF etc decreases body response to EPO

    Hypercoagulability¶

    Increased risk of DVT and PE due to

    • Extrinsic vascular compression / invasion

    • Tissue factor production by tumor and endothelial cells

    • Accentuated platelet activation and accumulation

    • Expression of phosyphatidyl serine that supports prothrombinase and tenase activity, inflammation-mediated increase in factor VIII, fibrinogen and von Willebrand factor

    • Impaired endogenous fibrinolysis due to excess level of plasminogen activator-inhibitor 1

    Hypercalcemia due to Malignancy¶

    Most common metabolic abnormality -- 10% of cancers

    • Osteoclast resorption of bone due to endocrine (PTH) and paracine (local factors from bone mets inc RANKL, IL-1, TNF-α, TGF-α) effects

    It occurs either in the presence of skeletal metastases or in their absence in patients who have humoural hypercalcemia of malignancy. Tumour derived parathyroid hormone related peptide plays a major role in both types. Common tumour types include myeloma, breast, renal, lung, and thyroid cancer. Symptoms range from mild to severe: nausea, anorexia, constipation, polyuria, thirst, lethargy, confusion, stupor, seizures, arrhythmias and coma.

    Decision to treat must take into consideration the potential benefits (improved symptoms) versus burdens of therapy (taking blood/administering fluids and medication); the patient's disease trajectory and the overall goals of care. Urgency of treatment depends on the severity of hypercalcemia and the associated symptoms.

    Suggested Workflow

    1. Check corrected calcium + renal function. Start IV hydration with 1-3L 0.9% NaCl for confirmed hypercalcemia and monitor Intake/Output to ensure negative balance. Stop calcium supplements. Eliminate precipitating factors

    2. Then for Severely symptomatic patient or corrected calcium > 3.5mmol/L: IV bisphosphonate

    3. Recheck calcium after 3-5days (or recheck earlier if calcitonin is used)

    4. At all times (until calcium has normalized), continue rehydration and await bisphosphonate effect (Bisphosphonates work within 1 week).

    5. Calcitonin use can be considered if patients are very symptomatic from hypercalcemia and the symptoms need to be controlled fast.

      • It can be started at SC 4IU/kg q12h. . Calcium should be rechecked after 1-2 days → If no response, consider increase calcitonin dose to 8IU/Kg q 12h → recheck calcium after another 1-2 days → if no response, increase calcitonin to 8IU/kg q6h.

      • NB: Calcitonin has a short onset and duration of action (hours) and with repeated doses (within 48hours) tachyphylaxis will develop and it becomes ineffective.

    6. Once calcium normal, hydration can be stopped. Continue monitoring of calcium; ensure precipitating factors eliminated.

    7. Asymptomatic/mildly symptomatic patients with corrected calcium 2.6 3.5mmol/L: recheck calcium/renal function 1 day after rehydration. If still raised >3mmol/L or patient still symptomatic - give IV bisphosphonate

    8. Recheck calcium after 3-5 days.

      • If normal: continue to monitor calcium levels; can stop hydration; ensure precipitating factors eliminated
      • If still elevated (but less than pre- treatment levels), continue hydration -> recheck after 2-3 days. Bisphosphonates can't be repeated until 7 days after first dose so as to avoid hypocalcemia.
      • If still elevated (and more than pre -treatment levels) - -> continue hydration. Consider adjuncts e.g. steroids in patients with hematological malignancies as they have anti-neoplastic effects. Consider repeating bisphosphonates and use of more potent bisphosphonates 7 days after the first dose has been given or if need for calcitonin treatment. Also consider if theres a possibility of anti-neoplastic treatment. Get expert advice.

    Notes regarding bisphosphonates

    • Most serious adverse effect is renal failure. Depending of degree of pre-existing renal failure, bisphosphonates might need dose reduction or might be contraindicated. They have poor oral bioavailability so the parenteral route is preferred.

    • Available agents are clodronate, pamidronate and zolendronate. Clodronate can be given po or s/c. Pamidronate and zolendronate are intravenous (though there are case reports of pamidronate being given s/c) and are commonly used in SGH. Relative potency of clodronate: pamidronate: zolendronate is 1:10:1000

    • Usual doses:

    • Clodronate: 1500mg given s/c or IV in 250-500ml NaCL over 4 hours as a single dose or 300mg once daily over 2-6hours(and not to be repeated for >7-10days); usual duration of effect: 2 weeks

    • Pamidronate: 30-90mg IV (in 250ml to 500ml NaCL over 60-90mins) (calcium 2.6 - 3mmol/L: 30mg; 3.0-3.5mmol/L: 60mg; >3.5mmol/L: 90mg); usual duration of effect: 3-4 weeks

    Superior Vena Cava Obstruction¶

    Superior Vena Cava Obstruction (SVCO) is due to compression or invasion of the SVC by mediastinal lymph nodes or tumour in the region of the right main bronchus. Severity of symptoms depends on degree and speed of narrowing of SVC.

    Causes

    • 90% caused by malignant tumours
    • 85% by lung cancer (small cell lung cancer, squamous-cell lung cancer) o Malignant lymphomas
    • < 2% thymoma or germ cell tumours
    • Other metastatic tumours
    • Non-malignant
    • Long term central venous catheters causing thrombus

    Presentation (Signs and Symptoms)

    • General inspection: facial swelling, peri-orbital edema, fixed distended neck veins
    • HEENT: Headache, dizziness, pressure in head and face, visual changes
    • Cardiorespiratory: cyanosis, dyspnea
    • Extremities: Upper limb oedema disproportionate to lower limb edema
    • Action: Pemberton sign

    Diagnosis

    • Mainly clinical
    • CT Scan most useful
    • Reveals site of obstruction and collateral flow
    • Differentiate extrinsic compression by tumour from thrombosis
    • Information about tumour mass
    • Guide for fine needle aspiration biopsy if histological diagnosis needed

    Management

    • Elevate head
    • Assessment of worsening neurological, pulmonary and cardiac function
    • No upper limb cannulas (acceptable on lower limbs)
    • Symptomatic treatment
    • Oxygen therapy and allay anxiety
    • Patients should be started on steroids as soon as possible (Dexamethasone 16mg daily)
    • Subsequently further treatment decisions will involve
    • radiologically-guided stenting (useful in severe respiratory distress) or radiotherapy.
    • Chemotherapy can be considered in patients with chemo sensitive tumours.
    • If the obstruction is due to a thrombus patients should be anti-coagulated unless contraindicated.

    Spinal Cord Compression¶

    Malignant spinal cord compression occurs when the dural sac and its contents are compressed at the level of the cord or cauda equina.

    • It affects about 35% of patients with cancer & 10% of patients with spinal metastases.
    • Cord compression can be the initial presentation of cancer.
    • Late diagnosis (more than 48hrs) is common causing permanent loss of function and significant morbidity.

    Signs & Symptoms

    • Back pain (present in 90% of cases) bandlike OR electricshock incident pain
    • Leg weakness
    • Increased reflexes (Upper motor neuron signs below level of compression)
    • Sensory level
    • Urinary hesitancy/bowel incontinence (late feature in SCC/early feature in cauda equina)

    Distribution

    • Thoracic spine 70%, as it has a smaller ratio of spinal canal to cord diameter than the other two spinal segments
    • Multiple contiguous levels 10% to 38%.
    • Lumbosacral spine 20%
    • Cervical spine 10%.

    Incidence in Malignancy

    • Lung 16%
    • Breast 12%
    • Unknown primary 11%
    • Lymphoma 11%
    • Myeloma 9%

    Assessment

    1. Rule out trauma: ask for any fall /injury to lower back
    2. Get a comprehensive pain history
    3. Please refer to pain section for information on pain assessment
    4. Perform a physical examination with emphasis on neurological assessment of the upper and lower limbs
    5. Assess power to determine myotomes involved
    6. Assess sensory level to determine dermatomal distribution
    7. Look out for spinal tenderness (can point towards a vertebral collapse)
    8. Assess urinary and bowel continence
    9. Any difficulties micturating/opening bowels
    10. Check for bladder and perform a per rectal examination to look for lax anal tone/saddle anesthesia
    11. CT / MRI scan
    12. Urgent referral for CT scans and MRI improves early detection (within 48hrs). MRI scans are more sensitive than CT Scans and are the gold standard for diagnosis.
    13. Usually recommend whole spine imaging in malignant disease because multiple levels can be at risk.

    Management

    • Start I.V. corticosteroids (IV Dexamethasone 16mg/24hrs)
    • Rationale: Shown to improve neurologic function and relieve pain by reducing oedema.
    • Assess Spinal Stability with Spinal Instability Neoplastic Score
    • Calculate Tokuhashi score: a prognostication tool that helps with decision-making regarding surgical intervention.
    • Assess extent of neurological deficits. If deficits are severe and >48hours, chance of neurological recovery is very low with RT or surgery. However RT may still be given for pain control if pain is an issue (in absence of spinal instability)
    • See table below for proposed management, if neurological deficits are mild OR < 48hours :
    • Urgent consultation by Radiation Oncologist (especially for radiosensitive tumour and no spinal instability) and Orthopedic/Spinal Surgeons (especially for spinal instability)
    • Rationale for RT: ICORG 05-03 and SCORAD III Randomised controlled trials have shown equivalence in terms of the proportion of patients ambulating after single fraction of RT versus multiple fractions. As such, unless patients have a longer survival, where tumour control with a longer course of RT is important, a single fraction should be given

    • Rationale for surgery: Surgery is the first choice where the site of the primary tumour is unknown. In cases of spinal instability or compression at a previously irradiated site, surgery is likely to give the best outcome but other factors such as ECOG status; prognosis and patients wishes need to be considered. Surgery should also be considered when neurological symptoms progress during radiotherapy, or where tumours are not radiosensitive.

    Treatment Related Conditions¶

    Tumor Lysis Syndrome¶

    • Defined biochemically as elevation in potassium, phosphate and uric acid and decrease in calcium level
    • Defined clinically when patient develops arrhythmia, acute kidney injury and etc
    • Risks of tumour lysis usually intermediate to high with aggressive lymphoma
    • Ensure all patients who are newly diagnosed or newly started on treatment,
    • Are on a 1.5 2L drip

    • Unless there are contraindications, they should be placed on allopurinol prophylaxis (300mg OM for those with normal renal function) at least for the first cycle of their treatment (usually for 14 days)

    • TLS bloods should be performed twice a day for the first few days when a patient is newly initiated on treatment. This frequency can be tailed down if patient remains stable.

    Neutropenic Fever or Sepsis¶

    Medical emergency.

    Management

    • Start Antibiotics
    • Ensure patient has broad spectrum antibiotics that covers for gram-negative and pseudomonas aeruginosae (e.g. tazocin, cefepime and amikacin) within 1 hour of the fever
    • Vancomycin is indicated in the following scenarios:
      • Hypotensive or severe sepsis
      • Suspicion of line sepsis (NOT ALL patient with a line and neutropenic sepsis needs vancomycin)
      • Severe mucositis (which increases risk of streptococci spp infection)
      • Pneumonia

    • Consider antifungal if the patient is persistently febrile after 48 hours of broad spectrum antibiotics or is neutropenic for more than 4 5 days

    • Ensure patient receives IV fluids (30mL/kg) within the first 1 hour

    • Start patient on GCSF 300mg even if the patient has had pegylated GCSF a few days ago
    • Granulocyte colony-stimulating factor, also known as colony-stimulating factor 3, is a glycoprotein that stimulates the bone marrow to produce granulocytes and stem cells and release them into the bloodstream.- Granulocyte-colony stimulating factor (G-CSF) mobilizes and increases the amount of hematopoietic stem cells in peripheral blood
    • The pegylated G-CSF has longer half-life and is given once only, which is more comfortable for patients, whereas the non-pegylated requires multiple daily injection because of its short half-life.

    Superior pulmonary sulcus (Pancoast) tumor¶

    Clinical Features

    • Shoulder pain due to invasion of the brachial plexus, pleura, ribs, or vertebral bodies. Because shoulder pain is common and often due to a benign cause (eg, osteoarthritis), the diagnosis of Pancoast tumor is frequently delayed.
    • Horner syndrome due to invasion of paravertebral sympathetic chain/stellate ganglion
    • Ipsilateral ptosis, miosis, enophthalmos & anhidrosis
    • Neurologic symptoms in the arm (invasion of C8-T2 nerves)
    • Weakness/atrophy of intrinsic hand muscles
    • Pain/paresthesia of 4th/5th digits & medial arm/forearm
    • in an ulnar distribution due to invasion of the inferior (C8-T1) portion of the brachial plexus
    • Supraclavicular lymphadenopathy
    • Hoarseness due to recurrent laryngeal nerve invasion
    • Superior vena cava syndrome
    • Weight loss is also common; pulmonary symptoms (eg, cough) do not typically occur until the tumor is large (as in this patient).

    Testicular Tumor¶

    Testicular Tumor Risk Factors The most common testicular tumor is germ cell tumor. The specific cause of germ cell tumors is unknown, but various factors have been associated with the increased risk.

    Genetics play a role in testicular cancer risk. Klinefelter's syndrome (47xxy) is associated with a higher incidence of germ cell tumors. For first degree relatives of individuals affected there is approximately six to ten- fold increased risk for germ cell tumors. Other conditions such as Down syndrome, testicular feminizing syndrome, true hermaphrodites, persistent mullerian syndrome, and cutaneous ichthyosis are at higher risk for developing germ cell tumors.

    Family history also plays an important role in testicular cancer risk. There have been reports of six-fold increased risk among male offspring of a patient with testicular cancer.

    Patients with cryptorchidism have 20 to 40-fold increased risk compared with their normal counterparts. Cryptorchidism is the absence of one or both testes from the scrotum, usually as the result of an undescended testis.Orchipexy, even at an early age, appears to reduce the incidence of germ cell tumor only slightly.

    Numerous environmental hazards, such as industrial occupations and drug exposures have been implicated in the development of testicular cancer. They include DES, Agent Orange, and solvents used to clean jets and ochratoxin A.

    One to two percent of patients with testicular cancer will develop a second primary cancer in the contralateral testicle. This represents a 500-fold increase in risk compared with normal population.

    Prior trauma, elevated scrotal temperature, and recurrent activities, such as horseback riding and motorcycle riding do not appear to be related to the development of testicular tumors.

    Testicular tumor presents as scrotal mass that is rarely accompanied by tenderness.

    The swelling is solid so should not transilluminate.

    A testicular tumor is usually non-tender to palpation

    Testicular cancer is the most common malignancy affecting males between the ages 15 and 35, although it accounts for only one percent of all cancers in men.

    These tumors could present as a nodule or as a painless swelling of the testicle, 30-40% may present with dull ache or heavy sensation in the lower abdomen, perianal area, or scrotum areas. Acute pain is the presenting symptom in ten percent of cases.

    There is no evidence to support routine screening for testicular cancer in asymptomatic adolescents and young adults.

    Three Types of Testicular Tumors Germ cell tumor (GCT)

    Seminomatous (SGCT)

    Nonseminomatous (NSGCT)

    Germ cell tumors (GCTs) are the most common-accounting for (95%) of primary testicular tumors-and are classified as either seminomas (45%) or others (50%), called nonseminomatous germ cell tumors, or NSGCT, based on histology.

    Distribution of nonseminomatous germ cell tumors (NSGCTs):

    Embryonal cell tumor, the classic pure-cell NSGCT (20%)

    Mixed GCTs (40%)

    Teratomas and teratocarcinomas (30%)

    Yolk sac tumors (also known as endodermal sinus tumors) are the most common prepubertal GCTs. They may be benign but are most often malignant. Most affected patients require surgery and chemotherapy because of the aggressive nature of the tumors, but the overall prognosis is excellent.

    Choriocarcinoma is the most lethal but least common NSGCT (1%)

    Non-germ cell tumors

    Non-germ cell tumors (Leydig cell tumors and Sertoli cell tumors) constitute the remaining 5% of primary testicular tumors; these are rare tumors that are malignant in only about 10% of the cases.

    Extragonadal

    Lymphoma, leukemia, and melanoma are the most common malignancies that metastasize to the testicle (extragonadal tumors)

    General¶

    Many patients are on ongoing chemotherapy, usually they cycle every 3 weeks, some every 4 weeks. You will get to know your lymphoma patients very well. Usually they are seen in clinic prior to their admission for chemotherapy. From clinic, consultants will fax off their chemo order protocol. Haem pharmacy (ext 4559) has a cut off time for chemo orders ie 4.30pm. The moment you sense patients on your radar, call haem pharmacy and check if they have the chemo order form. If not, you have to get hold of a registrar ASAP to write up the chemo for the patients.

    If patients are admitted a day or so after their clinic appointment, their NCC folder will be sent to ward 48 so you can retrieve it from there.

    All outpatient clinic notes (this is super handy) are documented in clindoc and rarely do you have to sweat over not having old notes and not knowing what your consultant wants. Most of the consultants will be quite clear in what they want for their patients admission and what their plans are for TCU, scans, readmission etc.

    Chemotherapy Regimes¶

    Only ward 48/72/77/78 have nurses trained in giving chemo. If you have a patient in overflows whom will require chemotherapy, get them to these wards and ask for height and weight to be done. This is required to calculate body surface area to dose chemo.

    BSA = square root of {[ Ht (cm) x Wt (kg) ] / 3600} (Motsellar formula)

    R-CHOP¶

    Commonest lymphoma = DLBCL

    RCHOP is pretty much standard of care for DLBCL. R = rituximab (CD 20 monoclonal antibody) C = cyclophosphamide H = doxorubicin **H**ydrochloride O = vincristine aka **O**ncovin & P = prednisolone

    What to watch for

    • Remember doxorubicin is a vesicant (as is vincristine). If it extravasates = extensive necrosis.

    • To avoid extravasation

      • Ensure there is a peripheral plug that is working well
      • Ensure good intact vein with good backflow of blood
      • Insertion site should not be distal to a recent venipuncture or in an arm with compromised circulation (think of requesting groin central line in SVCO)

    • If extravasates:

      • Doxorubicin: Stop injecting immediately → stop IV infusion → aspirate as much drug out as possible with clean syringe → apply DMSO 99% topical solution (4 drops per 10cm2 of skin) → allow DMSO to air dry → elevate limb and apply COLD compress
      • Vincristine: As above except no DMSO and apply WARM compress

      OBSERVE CLOSELY. CALL ONCO REG, CALL SURGEON IF WORSENING TISSUE INJURY

    • Rituximab infusion related reactions can be as high as 80% and can be severe. You will be called for urticaria, dyspnea, bronchospasm, angioedema, hypotension, tachycardia, back or abdo pain or discomfort or arrest.

    • If reaction is moderate: stop infusion → give Benadryl 25mg bolus → give hydrocortisone 100mg IV → if recovers resume as per protocol at a slower rate.

    • If reaction is severe i.e. respiratory distress or angioedema or hypotension → stop infusion and do not restart → IV Benadryl 50mg + IV hydrocortisone + Oxygen + fluids → Bronchodilators if bronchospasm → consider IM adrenaline 0.5mg

    A little about rituximab: Rituximab is extremely costly therefore in general we try to finish up the infusion. Please do not order for rituximab to be thrown away prior to checking with a Med Onco reg. Rituximab is only given if a tumour expresses CD 20+ (ie B Cell!!). If you see it written up for T cell, question it. Hold the chemo. Tell your consultant. If you catch this, youll have a super good lunch on us!!

    24 hours after RCHOP because it is a myelosuppressive regime, remember that gCSF or PEGgCSF has to be given. Please check that midcycle methotrexate is not to be given with the RCHOP prior to giving gCSF.

    IV METHOTREXATE¶

    • Preparation
    • Check FBC/Renal panel/Liver panel.
    • Ensure patient does not have any effusions as MTX can accumulate in them.
    • Hold off aspirin, omeprazole, Bactrim, penicillin and related antibiotics prior to giving MTX due to drug interactions.
    • Patients are hydrated with 3L of fluids (usually half strength NS) with 30 mmol of NaHCO3 in every 500mL of fluid. (cut down to 2L if patient has cardiac issues)
    • Monitoring
    • Check urine pH every shift. We aim urine pH >7. Write a standing order for IV sodium bicarbonate 30 mmol bolus q8H to given if urine pH 7 or less.
    • Check I/O every shift. We aim for shift I/O <500ml, daily I/O<800mL. Write a standing order for IV Lasix to be given if I > O by 500ml per shift or 800 ml per day. These patients will need to be handed over to the on-call for the urine pH to be traced and shift I/O to be < 500mL.
    • Logistics of Drug Delivery
    • IV MTX is usually given at 9am (ensure this is given before lunch by calling the hematology pharmacy the day before to ensure chemo prescription is received) and runs for 3 hours.
    • Ensure that methotrexate levels are ordered and checked at 24, 48 and 72 hours AFTER START of infusion ie 9 am days after chemo ends if chemo was given the day before. For the osteosarcoma patients, they will also have immediate end of infusion MTX levels measured this level can be ignored.
      • At the same time MTX levels are checked, also order IV folinic acid 30mg q6 hour IV to start 24 hours after end of infusion. (order it and suspend it until it is due)
      • MTX blocking some of the effects of folic acid. A folate deficiency may cause side effects such as mouth sores, stomach problems such as nausea or abdominal pain, liver problems or problems with producing blood cells.
    • We expect MTX levels to be 1 at 24 hours, 0.1 at 48 hours and <0.05 at 72 hours. The dose of folinic acid is adjusted according to the MTX levels therefore call your registrars with the MTX levels so that they can adjust dose of folinic acid.
    • If MTX levels are between 0.05 - 0.1, send the patient home with PO folinic acid 15mg q6H for 6 doses.
    • GCSF can be given if MTX levels are < 0.1.

    Cisplatin¶

    This is nephrotoxic and causes many electrolyte disturbance due to tubular damage. Ensure electrolytes and renal function are checked. Do ensure that the patient has IV fluids with electrolyte replacement ordered up. Refer to the protocols.

    Most of the lymphoma regimes are protocolled so look out for the chemo protocol. In ward 48/78 it is in a clear pocket in front of the file, in ward 72/77 it is in a separate file. Follow what it suggests especially with hydration and prophylactic Bactrim, acyclovir, antifungals, antibiotics etc. Dont forget the delayed anti emetics. A puking patient is NOT a happy patient!

    Ifosfamide eg in ICE / IVAC¶

    Ensure urine dip stick done for blood every shift. Acrolein which is a metabolite of ifosfamide irritates the bladder lining causing haematuria. Ensure mesna which inactivates acrolein is being given.

    General Notes¶

    Lines

    Several of our chemo protocols require infusional chemo or patients have veins so delicate that a central line either PICC or Hickman line is required.

    • If PICC is ordered, order a power PICC line, non tunneled, double lumen.
    • If Hickman order a non tunneled double lumen.
